About Me

Research Interests: Galaxy Formation and Evolution, Dark Matter, Small scale structure formation, Cosmological Simulations

I study the formation and evolution of galaxies and galaxy clusters using a combination of cosmological simulations and observational data. My work spans multiple scales, from individual stars in nearby galaxies to galaxy clusters in the Local Universe.

Current Research

  • Intracluster Light in Galaxy Clusters: At the University Observatory Munich (DRAGONS group), I am working with zoom-in constrained simulations of Local Universe clusters to study the diffuse intracluster light (ICL) and its connection to cluster dynamics.
  • Variable Sources in IC 10: I am building a U-band catalog of variable sources in IC 10, a starburst dwarf galaxy in the Local Group, using ~5 years of weekly Swift UVOT data to explore how massive stars influence the interstellar medium.

Previous Work

  • Fuzzy-Gasoline Simulations: I explored how Fuzzy Dark Matter (FDM) shapes dwarf galaxy evolution using 30 high-resolution zoom-in simulations with FUZZY-GASOLINE, a modified GASOLINE2 code. The simulations include both hydrodynamical and dark matter-only runs, capturing baryonic processes—metal cooling, star formation, supernova feedback, and black hole physics—alongside the Madelung formulation of FDM. We found that FDM produces cored dark matter profiles in all haloes, especially in low-mass systems at high redshift, while baryons form cores in a specific mass range at low redshift. Despite these differences, stellar observables like star formation histories and velocity dispersions closely resemble Cold Dark Matter predictions, making it challenging to distinguish the two models through stars alone. We published our findings on the arXiv in November 2024 (arXiv:2411.09733).

Research Collaborations

I am currently working with members of the DRAGONS group at the University Observatory Munich (USM) on zoom-in constrained simulations of Local Universe galaxy clusters. I previously collaborated with the Galaxy Formation Group (GFG) at NYU Abu Dhabi, working with Dr. Andrea Valerio Macciò and Dr. Matteo Nori on Fuzzy-Gasoline simulations, and with Dr. Joseph Gelfand at the Center for Astrophysics and Space Science (CASS) on observational studies of stellar populations and variable stars in IC 10.

Fuzzy-Gasoline

Research Summary

During my undergraduate studies, I actively engaged in projects centered on galaxy formation and cosmological simulations, which deepened my understanding of structure formation models. In the Spring of 2023, I joined Prof. Andrea Macciò's Galaxy Formation Group, where I contributed to the FUZZY-GASOLINE project, analyzing 30 zoom-in simulations utilizing the Fuzzy Dark Matter (FDM) framework. Collaborating with Dr. Matteo Nori, I developed and refined analysis pipelines, leveraging parallel computing techniques, and independently executed simulations on the Jubail HPC Linux cluster at NYU Abu Dhabi. My focus shifted to interpreting simulation outputs that spanned redshifts from z = 99 to z = 0, revealing the distinct impacts of FDM and baryonic processes on dark matter dynamics and observable properties in galaxies. Notably, we found that FDM-induced core formation occurs predominantly in low-mass systems at high redshift, while baryon-driven cores emerge in massive galaxies at lower redshifts, with implications for distinguishing between FDM and Cold Dark Matter (CDM) models.

Since graduating in the Spring of 2024, I have been leading the Fuzzy-HELLO project that entails the creation of high-redshift (z ~ 3) Fuzzy-Gasoline simulations inspired by the HELLO suite, both of which share the NIHAO suite of simulations as their progenitor. The aim of this project is to explore Fuzzy-Gasoline systems at higher redshifts, incorporating additional physics (local photoionization feedback, black hole feedback, and updated modules for chemical enrichment) and achieving higher resolutions. This approach allows us to leverage the weaker baryonic feedback and stronger FDM Quantum Potential present at these early times. Since this situation would exacerbate the dark matter core-cusp differentiator evident in the low-to-mid-mass, dark matter dominated Fuzzy-Gasoline dwarf galaxy systems, we expect to observe some remnants of this phenomenon in its observable properties, particularly its star formation histories.
