Research Interests: Galaxy Formation and Evolution, Numerical Astrophysics, Dark Matter, Large Scale Structure

My research interests center on numerical cosmological simulations, specifically, the formation of large-scale structures in the Universe. This primarily includes modelling and analyzing the effects of Fuzzy Dark Matter (FDM) cosmologies on small-scale structure formation using Zoom-in Gasoline SPH simulations at z∼0. Additionally, I have previously worked on creating machine learning models that predict planetary habitability in binary star systems.

Fuzzy-Gasoline

Research Summary

During my undergraduate studies, I actively engaged in projects centered on galaxy formation and cosmological simulations, which deepened my understanding of structure formation models. In the Spring of 2023, I joined Prof. Andrea Macciò's Galaxy Formation Group, where I contributed to the FUZZY-GASOLINE project, analyzing 30 zoom-in simulations utilizing the Fuzzy Dark Matter (FDM) framework. Collaborating with Dr. Matteo Nori, I developed and refined analysis pipelines, leveraging parallel computing techniques, and independently executed simulations on the Jubail HPC Linux cluster at NYU Abu Dhabi. My focus shifted to interpreting simulation outputs that spanned redshifts from z = 99 to z = 0, revealing the distinct impacts of FDM and baryonic processes on dark matter dynamics and observable properties in galaxies. Notably, we found that FDM-induced core formation occurs predominantly in low-mass systems at high redshift, while baryon-driven cores emerge in massive galaxies at lower redshifts, with implications for distinguishing between FDM and Cold Dark Matter (CDM) models.

Since graduating in the Spring of 2024, I have been leading the Fuzzy-HELLO project that entails the creation of high-redshift (z ~ 3) Fuzzy-Gasoline simulations inspired by the HELLO suite, both of which share the NIHAO suite of simulations as their progenitor. The aim of this project is to explore Fuzzy-Gasoline systems at higher redshifts, incorporating additional physics (local photoionization feedback, black hole feedback, and updated modules for chemical enrichment) and achieving higher resolutions. This approach allows us to leverage the weaker baryonic feedback and stronger FDM Quantum Potential present at these early times. Since this situation would exacerbate the dark matter core-cusp differentiator evident in the low-to-mid-mass, dark matter dominated Fuzzy-Gasoline dwarf galaxy systems, we expect to observe some remnants of this phenomenon in its observable properties, particularly its star formation histories.
